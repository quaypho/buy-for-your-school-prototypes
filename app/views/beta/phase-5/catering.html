Things to consider when procuring catering services

When you're finding the right procurement process to follow, you'll need to consider things like how much you're spending and how much time you have.

Lifetime cost of the catering contract

Catering contracts usually last 3 to 5 years, but that can vary. It's unlikely that the lifetime cost of the service will be low enough for you to choose a single supplier directly from a framework. You'll probably need to get at least 3 quotes, advertise a contract and run a procurement process, or run an EU compliant procurement process.

There are 3 catering services frameworks available that you can use to run a mini competition, which involves asking 3 suppliers on the framework to quote.

Time needed to run a catering procurement

When deciding your process, consider how much time you have to spend on the procurement. For example, running a compliant procurement process for catering can take around 6 months for a person or team with enough time to work on the procurement most days. Running your own procurement can also be a complex process, with a lot of different things to consider, but there is additional support available alongside this manual if you get stuck.

Schools can work with their local authority or suppliers, but running the service in-house is another option. You may want to consider whether this is right for your school before you procure a contract. There are benefits and challenges to running the service yourself.

Benefits

You might be able to:

  • increase uptake of school meals
  • negotiate better prices from suppliers
  • increase food quality
  • respond quicker if your equipment is broken or faulty
  • develop staff skills through bespoke training
  • generate income by offering your catering services to other schools or nurseries
  • be in control of your expenditure

Challenges

You may want to consider:

  • how to provide training on food specifications, menu building, health and safety, food hygiene
  • costs involved in staffing, equipment, food and utilities, maintenance of equipment
  • how to keep staff up to date with legislation and regulations
  • how to manage demand by getting regular feedback from parents, pupils and governors
  • TUPE and LGPS

You’ll need to be aware of some of the rules, regulations and requirements that can apply to a catering contract.

Your school must:

  • comply with Standards for school food in England
  • advertise a contract notice on the Find a Tender service if lifetime value of your contract is over the procurement threshold
  • consider whether TUPE and LGPS will apply, especially if you’re looking to outsource or come away from your local authority for the first time
  • confirm from suppliers that staff have had the relevant DBS checks.

Read about how to write a specification

Below are some of the things that you should include. You can also use our tool to create a specification.

Objectives 

Your objectives are important because it helps a supplier to decide whether they are a good fit for your school. Include:

  • your mission statement
  • goals your school has 
  • policies that relate to catering
  • objectives that relates to social value

Contract management

Set out how you expect the contract to be managed to ensure a good working relationship. Include:

  • your requirements on how the contract should be managed, such as any review meetings, how to monitor performance or costs, how complaints are managed and so on
  • what operational overheads the supplier will cover
  • any staff that the supplier will need to provide, and staff that need to be transferred

Service parameters

Tell suppliers what service you want them to provide and who for. Include:

  • when you want the service to start
  • current services types, and times they are provided
  • customer numbers
  • free school meal numbers
  • the services you would like the supplier to provide

Menu and ordering

Give suppliers some information about the food you would like them to serve and how it should be ordered and paid for. Include:

  • any requirements for the food served
  • ingredients or allergens to be avoided
  • ordering and payment systems you have, or would like to be provided
  • who is responsible for any dinner money debt

Facilities

Outline the spaces and equipment that you have available for use. Include:

  • the condition of any kitchens and if they are ready for service
  • equipment available to a supplier
  • a description of the dining areas
  • who sets up and clears away before and after service
  • cleaning responsibilities

Social value in catering services

When deciding how to weight social value in your evaluation, consider the unique ways a catering service can help your school meet the social value aims.

For example, catering suppliers in particular may be able to play a role in:

  • Fighting climate change - by avoiding food waste through their menu designs and having robust recycling and waste management procedures
  • Tackling workforce inequality - by paying a fair wage and avoiding zero-hour contracts for catering staff
  • Improving health and wellbeing - by providing healthy meals for pupils, contributing to the curriculum to get pupils interested in food, or hosting cooking classes for parents
